## Historical Background

- The church must be *pure*—a community of the holy

## "Apologist" in the Christian Sense

An **apologist** (from Greek *apologia*, meaning "defense" or "reasoned argument") is someone who offers a rational defense of the faith—explaining and justifying Christian belief to skeptics, doubters, and outsiders.

It's not "apologizing" in the modern sense of saying sorry. It's *making a case*.

## What C.S. Lewis Did

Lewis is called an apologist because he:

- **Argued for Christianity's reasonableness** to a secular, skeptical audience (especially post-war Britain)
- **Used logic, analogy, and imagination** rather than appeals to authority or scripture alone
- **Addressed objections head-on**—the problem of evil, the reliability of miracles, the claims of other religions
- **Wrote for ordinary people**, not theologians—*Mere Christianity*, *The Problem of Pain*, *Miracles*

His famous "trilemma" (Jesus was either Liar, Lunatic, or Lord) is classic apologetics—a logical argument meant to corner the skeptic into taking Christ's claims seriously.

## The Contrast with Augustine

Here's where it connects to our conversation:

Lewis was an apologist who *persuaded*. He made arguments. He invited. He let readers decide. His own conversion was reluctant—"the most dejected and reluctant convert in all England"—and he extended that same patience to others.

Augustine started as a persuader too. But he became a *compeller*. When argument failed, he turned to force. "Compel them to come in."

Both were brilliant defenders of the faith. But they modeled very different relationships between belief and coercion.

Lewis let the rich young ruler walk away. Augustine sent the imperial guard after him.
